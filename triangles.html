The Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT) / Thales Theorem

The BPT is the first key to unlocking Similarity. It states that if a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle to intersect the other two sides in distinct points, the other two sides are divided in the same ratio. It's the law of proportion within a stable structure.

In $\triangle ABC$, if $DE \parallel BC$, then:

$$\large \frac{AD}{DB} = \frac{AE}{EC}$$

The parts created by the parallel line maintain a beautiful, equal ratio.

Criteria for Similarity (The Three Tests)

To prove two triangles are similar, we don't need to measure every side and angle. We only need to pass one of these three elegant tests:

  • **AA (Angle-Angle):** If two angles of one triangle are equal to two angles of another, the third angles must also be equal, making the shapes identical.
  • **SSS (Side-Side-Side):** If the ratio of all three corresponding sides is equal, the shapes are similar.
  • **SAS (Side-Angle-Side):** If the ratio of two corresponding sides is equal, and the included angle is also equal, the shapes are similar.

The ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the square of the ratio of their corresponding sides:

$$\large \frac{\text{Area}(\triangle ABC)}{\text{Area}(\triangle PQR)} = \left(\frac{AB}{PQ}\right)^2$$

Pythagoras Theorem: The Sacred Rule of Right Triangles

The most famous rule for stability. In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the longest side, the 'resultant path') is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (the 'vertical and horizontal efforts').

For a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$, and hypotenuse $c$:

$$\large a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$
