Chapter 9: Applications of Trigonometry

The Surveyor‚Äôs Tale: Using Sight to Understand Height and Distance

If Chapter 8 gave us the language (the Ratios), Chapter 9 gives us the practical map. This chapter is about using the language of angles to solve real-world problems involving heights and distances. We translate a physical scenario into a Right Triangle, and then we use the appropriate trigonometric ratio to find the missing side.

Angle of Elevation (Looking Up)

This is the angle formed between your **Horizontal Line of Sight** and your actual **Line of Sight** when you look **up** at an object. If you are standing on the ground and look up at a kite or the top of a building, that is the angle of elevation.

Angle of Depression (Looking Down)

This is the angle formed between the **Horizontal Line of Sight** (from the elevated position) and the actual **Line of Sight** when you look **down** at an object. Remember, due to geometry rules, the Angle of Depression from the top of the tower to a point on the ground is always **equal** to the Angle of Elevation from that point on the ground back to the tower!

In most application problems, we know the **distance on the ground (Adjacent)** and need the **height (Opposite)**, or vice-versa. Since the Tangent ratio relates Opposite and Adjacent, it is the most frequently used tool for solving real-world height and distance problems:

$$\large \tan \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}$$
