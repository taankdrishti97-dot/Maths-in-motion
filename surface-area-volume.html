Chapter 12: Surface Areas and Volumes

The Three Dimensions: Measuring the Skin and Soul of Objects

In this chapter, we enter the world of three-dimensional space, where objects possess **depth**. We seek to quantify two properties:

  • **Surface Area (The Skin):** The measure of the exposed outer boundary or 'skin' of the object. It is a 2D measure, calculated in square units (e.g., $cm^2$).
  • **Volume (The Soul):** The measure of the space occupied by the object, representing what it can hold inside. It is a 3D measure, calculated in cubic units (e.g., $cm^3$).

Core Formulas for Independent Solids

The foundation rests on the correct formulas for the primary 3D shapes.

1. Cuboid (Length $l$, Breadth $b$, Height $h$):

$$\text{TSA} = 2(lb + bh + hl)$$ $$\text{Volume} = l \times b \times h$$

2. Cylinder (Radius $r$, Height $h$):

$$\text{CSA} = 2\pi rh$$ $$\text{TSA} = 2\pi r(h + r)$$ $$\text{Volume} = \pi r^2 h$$

3. Cone (Radius $r$, Height $h$, Slant Height $l$):

$$\text{Slant Height }(l) = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}$$ $$\text{CSA} = \pi r l$$ $$\text{TSA} = \pi r (l + r)$$ $$\text{Volume} = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h$$

4. Sphere (Radius $r$):

$$\text{Surface Area} = 4\pi r^2$$ $$\text{Volume} = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3$$

5. Hemisphere (Radius $r$):

$$\text{CSA} = 2\pi r^2$$ $$\text{TSA} = 3\pi r^2$$ $$\text{Volume} = \frac{2}{3} \pi r^3$$

Combination of Solids: The Art of Assembly

The main challenge is calculating the properties of objects created by joining two or more basic solids (e.g., a cone placed on a cylinder).

When dealing with combined solids, the rules are different for Area and Volume:

Rule for Volume (The Soul):

When solids are joined, their souls (volumes) are simply **additive**. The total volume is the sum of the volumes of the individual parts, as space is never lost or gained in a combination.

$$\large \text{Total Volume} = \text{Volume}_1 + \text{Volume}_2$$
Rule for Surface Area (The Skin):

The total surface area is **NOT** the sum of the individual surface areas. When two solids are joined, the area where they touch is hidden and must be **excluded**. You must always calculate the sum of the **Curved Surface Areas (CSAs)** or the **exposed faces** of the individual parts.

$$\large \text{Total SA} = \text{CSA}_1 + \text{CSA}_2$$
