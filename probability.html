Chapter 14: Probability

Predicting the Future: The Art of Knowing the Odds

Probability is a measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. It is the core mathematical tool for handling uncertainty, allowing us to quantify our expectations. All probability values must fall within the range of $0$ (impossible event) and $1$ (sure event).

The Core Definition of Probability

The probability of an event $E$, denoted $P(E)$, is a ratio that compares the number of successful outcomes (favorable to the event) to the total possible outcomes (the sample space).

$$\large P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of Favourable Outcomes}}{\text{Total Number of Possible Outcomes}}$$

The probability $P(E)$ is always between 0 and 1, inclusive: $0 \le P(E) \le 1$.

Complementary Events: The Opposite Outcome

Every event has a **complement**‚Äîthe event that it does **not** happen. If $E$ is the event, its complement is denoted as $\bar{E}$ (or $E'$). The probability of an event and the probability of its complement must always sum to $1$, as one or the other is guaranteed to occur.

The sum of the probabilities of an event and its complement is $1$:

$$\large P(E) + P(\bar{E}) = 1$$

Therefore, to find the probability of the complement:

$$\large P(\bar{E}) = 1 - P(E)$$

Key Terminology and Certainty

  • **Elementary Event:** An outcome of an experiment that has only one single result (e.g., getting a '3' on a dice roll).
  • **Impossible Event:** An event that can never happen. Its probability is **$0$**. (e.g., Rolling an 8 on a standard six-sided die.)
  • **Sure (Certain) Event:** An event that is guaranteed to happen. Its probability is **$1$**. (e.g., Rolling a number less than 7 on a standard six-sided die.)
