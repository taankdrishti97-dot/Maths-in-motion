Chapter 10: Circles

The Perfect Boundary: The Eternal Connection (Tangent and Radius)

A circle is the set of all points that are equidistant from a fixed central point. It represents perfection, balance, and boundary. In this chapter, we explore how lines interact with this perfect boundary, defining three possibilities: the **Non-intersecting Line**, the **Secant** (which cuts through), and the **Tangent** (which just grazes).

[Aesthetic diagram of a circle with a radius drawn to a point of tangency, clearly marking the right angle]

The Tangent-Radius Theorem (The Eternal Connection)

A **Tangent** is a line that meets the circle at exactly one point, $P$. This theorem reveals the single most profound relationship in the chapter: the radius drawn to the point of contact ($P$) is **always perpendicular** to the tangent at that point. This perfect $90^\circ$ angle is the eternal truth of the tangent's boundary.

Theorem 10.1: The tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.

$$\large \text{Radius } \perp \text{ Tangent at } P$$

Length of Tangents (The External Witness)

If you draw two tangents to a circle from a single point outside the circle, $A$, the lengths of those two tangent segments ($AP$ and $AQ$) are always equal. The circle insists on symmetry, ensuring that the influence of any external witness is perfectly balanced in all directions.

Theorem 10.2: The lengths of tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal.

$$\large AP = AQ$$

Parallel Tangents (The Perfect Pair)

A circle can have infinitely many tangents, but it can only have a maximum of two parallel tangents at any given moment. These two tangents must be drawn at the endpoints of a single **diameter**, perfectly opposing each other across the center.
